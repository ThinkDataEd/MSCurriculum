##**<u>Lesson 7: What's in a Question?</u>**


###**Objective:**
Students will be able to differentiate between statistical questions and survey questions. They will practice formulating both types of questions and understand the characteristics of good statistical questions. Students will be introduced to the Pose Questions phase of the Data Cycle.


###**Materials:**
1. Data Cycle poster [pages 2 & 3] ([LMR_U1_L01_B_The_Data_Cycle](../MSDS_Curriculum/2_MSDS_LMRs/MSDS_LMR_Unit_1/LMR_U1_L01_B.pdf)) 

2. Statistical Questions Peer Review ([LMR_U1_L07_A_Statistical_Questions_Peer_Review](../MSDS_Curriculum/2_MSDS_LMRs/MSDS_LMR_Unit_1/LMR_U1_L07_A.pdf)) 

###**Vocabulary:**
[Pose Questions (phase)](../../vocabulary/unit1/#pose-questions-phase "the phase of the Data Cycle that generally starts an investigation by creating a statistical question; statistical questions address variability and can be answered with data"){ .md-button }
[statistical questions](../../vocabulary/unit1/#statistical-questions "a question that leads to many different answers when you ask an entire group of people or measure a group of things; a key feature is that the answer varies (or changes) for each person or item in the group"){ .md-button }

###**Essential Concepts:**

!!! note "Essential Concepts: "
    Good questions are the starting point for any data investigation. The type of question asked determines the type of data needed and the scope of the investigation.

    100. Pose a simple question to students and ask them to come up with a single response based on their own experience. Suggested question: How many different states in the US have you visited?

    100. You will ask students to give their answers quickly using one of two visual methods: (1) Stand and Share, or (2) Four Corners.

        100. Method 1: Stand and Share – Ask for groups to stand up based on their answer. Quickly call out the ranges and tally the results on the board.
            100. Ask “Who has been to 1 or 2 US states?” (Count hands)

            100. Ask “Who has been to 3 or 4 US states?” (Count hands)

            100. Ask “Who has been to 5 or 6 US states?” (Count hands)

            100. Ask “Who has been to more than 6 US states?” (Count hands)

        100. Method 2: Four Corners – Have locations in the room for the various responses and have students move to that part of the room.

    100. Quick Discussion: Point to the collected counts on the board and ask, “Did everyone give the same answer to my question?” The answer will very likely be no.

        100. We have a lot of different answers, which means we have variation! 

        100. We needed to collect data from each student in our class to see how we are similar and/or different. 

        100. Explain that today’s lesson will help us identify and create questions that have many answers

    <center><h3>Concept Development</h3></center>

    <b><i><span style="font-size: 18px";>Part 1: Introducing Statistical Questions</span></b></i>

7. Inform students that good detectives know that asking the right questions is one of the most important steps when solving a case. In a statistical investigation, we call these **Statistical Questions**. Share the definition below and have students record it in their notebooks. 

    100. **Statistical Question**: A Statistical Question is a question that leads to many different answers when you ask an entire group of people or measure a group of things. The key feature is that the answer varies (or changes) for each person or item in the group. Statistical questions are all about figuring out the patterns and characteristics of a whole group by measuring this variation.

    100. Point out that having variability in answers is what makes a question statistical. A good way to determine if a question is a statistical question is to ask: “Do I need data from multiple individuals to answer this question?”

8. Display the following 2 questions on the board:

    100. Question A: What is your favorite class? [Think about if you were Max from the suspect data cards; how would he answer this question?]

    100. Question B: What is the favorite class of the suspects in the DSU data set?

9. Engage students in a Think-Ink-Pair-Share discussion about the differences they notice between Question A and Question B. Some guiding questions are provided here, and sample answers are given in the next step. 

    100. Who would you ask each question to? 

    100. What kind of answer would you expect?

    100. How many responses would you need to collect to fully answer the question?

10. After pairs have discussed, bring the class back together and have a few volunteers share what differences they identified. Sample answers and ideas are provided below:

    100. Question A: 

        100. *Sample answer: Question A is asked about one person.* 

        100. *Sample answer: Question A has one answer (Max’s favorite class is Social Studies).*

    100. Question B: 

        100. *Sample answer: Question B is about the entire suspect list.*

        100. *Sample answer: Question B needs many answers to figure out a consensus on which class everyone likes.*
        
        100. *Sample answer: For Question B, we would expect different suspects to have different favorite classes (variability). We need many responses to come up with an answer.*

        100. *Sample answer: Question B might have a summary answer (“most suspects like X class”).*

11. As students share out their findings, record some of the key differences on the board and have students do the same in their notebooks.

12. Explain that Question A and Question B are examples of Survey Questions and Statistical Questions. Ask students to make an educated guess about which question type matches Question A and which type matches Question B. *Answer: Question A is a survey question and Question B is a statistical question.*

13. Solidify the difference between survey questions and statistical questions by connecting them back to what the students observed during the Think-Ink-Pair-Share activity.

            100. Question A is an example of a Survey Question because it asks about one person (Max). 

            100. A survey question is what we might ask a witness during an interview for a case.

            100. The person responding can only give one specific value as their answer. For example, Max cannot choose English and Math as his favorite class.

    100. Statistical Questions: These are questions that require us to collect many observations before we can provide an answer.

    100. Once student teams have created their 2 statistical questions, they will swap papers with another group and engage in a Peer Review. Explain that detectives often have their work checked by colleagues to test their suspicions, so we want our detective classmates to check our questions before we interview any suspects. For each statistical question proposed, the review team must answer the following 3 questions and provide feedback:

        100. Is it a statistical question? [Does it require collecting data from many cases, and do we expect the answers to vary?]
        
        100. Is it relevant to our investigation? [Will answering this question lead us closer to identifying the Candy Culprit?]
        
        100. What additional feedback do you have for this group?

    100. Once the peer reviews are complete, the teams will return their papers to each other to discuss the feedback they received and make any necessary edits to their statistical questions.
